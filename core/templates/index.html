Citadel Exzellenz Group · Doctrine

The Meridian Standard.

The discipline of operating between Africa and Europe, to one standard, delivered by a faculty, not a founder.

One reference line · One operating headquarters

ESMT Berlin trains Europeans. Meirc trains the Gulf. ESRI engineers for North American utilities. The leaders, engineers, and operators who move capital, energy, and data across the Africa–Europe meridian have been asked to assemble their own training, tools, and frameworks from institutions designed for someone else.

Citadel Exzellenz Group was founded to close that gap — and to close it to a specific standard.

The Meridian Standard is the discipline of operating at the intersection of earth sciences, geospatial intelligence, engineering, and leadership: delivered with German process rigor, Scottish academic credibility, and Chevron-grade operational realism, and explainable to any engineer or regulator who audits it.

Its Author

Dr. Onyedika Anthony Igbokwe began as a field geologist in northern Nigeria in 2005. An Erasmus Mundus Scholarship took him across Germany, Spain, and Portugal, where his Master's thesis applied AI to oil sands data a decade before AI became a corporate keyword. A Commonwealth Scholarship at Loughborough, a further Master's and a PhD at Ruhr University Bochum, fourteen peer-reviewed publications, an Assistant Professorship at the University of St Andrews, and a £52,500 Shell SPDC consultancy followed.

Five countries. Five degrees. Field, academy, and industry. The biography is not the offering — it is the evidence that whoever authored the Standard had to have stood in all three rooms.

Its Custodians

The Standard is now held by a faculty — practitioners drawn from the University of St Andrews, Oxford, Manchester, Bochum, Cambridge, and over a quarter-century of combined service at Chevron Nigeria — covering leadership development, executive coaching, organisational behaviour, geospatial and UAV intelligence, asset integrity, and offshore engineering between them.

Five disciplines. Six practitioners. Two operating divisions. One standard that is not negotiable.
